Government Policies

The government policies for marine organisms should aim to protect living marine resources and ensure environmental protection, water quality, and healthy oceans. The major things that should be considered include regulations, sustainability, and animal welfare. The government can help farmers by providing support through various programs and initiatives such as Whole-Farm Revenue Protection (WFRP), the Regional Aquaculture Center (RAC) program, and research investment. It is important to note that many government subsidies lead to overfishing, which can harm marine ecosystems and fisheries. Therefore, government policy should also aim to reduce harmful subsidies and promote sustainable fishing practices.

For example the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (MSA). The MSA promotes fishery conservation and economic sustainability through science-based catch limits, accountability measures, limited access programs, and increased international cooperation, ensuring the long-term biological and economic sustainability of marine fisheries for the environment and livelihoods.

Similarly, farmers can employ to limit fertiliser runoff. They can improve their drainage systems, plant cover crops to prevent erosion, plant trees, shrubs, and grasses along the edges of fields to act as a buffer, reduce the frequency and intensity of their tilling, keep their livestock out of nearby waterways so they don't transport nutrients, and, perhaps most importantly, apply fertiliser correctly.

Additionally, governments need to implement policies that recognize the importance of microorganisms in climate change and address carbon and nitrogen fluxes. An immediate, sustained, and concerted effort is required to explicitly include microorganisms in research, technology development, and policy and management decisions. Microorganisms not only contribute to the rate of climate change but can also contribute immensely to its effective mitigation and our adaptation tools. The statement also highlights the need to understand microbial interactions to help design measures to mitigate and control climate change and its effects.

Governments should also fund and assist farmers in their efforts because many of them demand an upfront investment, possibly some training, and it is more cost-effective to prevent dangerous algal blooms than to try to recover from them, not to mention the harm they can do to the environment and human health. Agricultural runoff might be regulated even if the Clean Water Act already had some rules in place. Governments can assist populations with lower incomes that are affected by algal blooms in a number of ways. There are a number of possibilities in this regard, including passing legislation to stop utilities from turning off the water to consumers who refuse to pay, giving water filters, educating people about toxic algal blooms, and generally making water more affordable.

GLOBAL GOVERNANCE

Marine pollution and loss of microbes are critical environmental issues requiring global governance. The EPA and UNEA play crucial roles in combating marine pollution, but the current governance landscape is inadequate to achieve the ambition of ending plastic pollution. Effective responses require involving state and non-state actors, businesses, and civil society. The UNEA adopted resolution 5/14 in March 2022, calling for an international agreement to address plastic pollution. The EPA helps shape U.S. government positions on international marine pollution issues, ensuring international decisions and management support the EPA's mission. Ocean governance is crucial in addressing marine pollution, involving various stakeholders such as governments, international organisations, civil society, and the private sector. To improve global governance, coordinated action is needed, strengthening legal instruments and institutional frameworks, promoting information exchange and regional cooperation, and developing a holistic approach based on integrated management and ecosystem approach. Increased public awareness and engagement are also essential for ocean stakeholders. Microbiome-based strategies can mitigate biodiversity decline and help address the complex challenges facing the oceans.

Development of new technology

Plastic pollution has become a major environmental concern due to the alarming concentration levels of plastics in our surroundings. Traditional methods of plastic degradation such as photo-oxidative, thermal, Ozone, mechanochemical, and catalytic approaches are costly and not suitable for low plastic concentrations. However, microbial use has emerged as an eco-friendly and efficient method for plastic degradation. To achieve this, it is important to investigate the potential of microbes including bacteria, fungi, and algae. Optimum conditions for these microbes must be maintained to ensure efficient plastic extermination. Additionally, a consortium of aerobic and anaerobic bacteria can be used for more effective plastic degradation, and successive use of microbes can also be implemented. Enzymes such as laccase, lignin-degrading enzymes, urease, lipase, and protease can be exploited for plastic degradation under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions. Implementing microbial consortia, their processes, and enzymes can be an effective and sustainable strategy for plastic degradation.
