Interconnectedness between microorganisms and climate change

Microorganisms play a crucial role in maintaining a healthy global ecosystem and are essential for the existence of all higher trophic life forms. Climate change is impacting most life on Earth, including microorganisms. Microorganisms have key roles in carbon and nutrient cycling, animal and plant health, agriculture, and the global food web. Changes in microbial biodiversity and activities will affect the resilience of all other organisms and hence their ability to respond to climate change. Harmful microorganisms can cause diseases in humans, animals, and plants. Anthropogenic climate change exacerbates the global spread of vector-borne pathogens and their diseases, increases antimicrobial resistance of microorganisms, and stresses marine life, causing disease and disrupting normal ecosystem function.

Human activities have a direct impact on microorganisms and contribute to greenhouse gas emissions. Deforestation, agriculture, industrial activities, and pollution all affect microbial communities and contribute to climate change. Deforestation reduces carbon dioxide absorption by trees and affects soil microbial communities, which play a crucial role in nutrient cycling and carbon storage. Agriculture contributes to greenhouse gas emissions through the use of fertilisers, which release nitrous oxide during microbial processes, and livestock farming, which produces methane. Industrial activities, such as the burning of fossil fuels, release carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. Pollution from human activities, such as oil spills and wastewater treatment, can affect microbial communities and contribute to greenhouse gas emissions. It's crucial to understand the role of microorganisms in greenhouse gas emissions and incorporate this knowledge into research, technology development, and policy and management decisions for effective mitigation of climate change.

The impact of elevated greenhouse gas concentrations on ocean temperature, acidification, stratification, mixing, thermohaline circulation, nutrient supply, irradiation, and extreme weather events affects the marine microbiota in ways that have substantial environmental consequences, including major shifts in productivity, marine food webs, carbon export, and burial in the seabed. Marine phytoplankton performs half of the global photosynthetic CO2 fixation and half of the oxygen production, despite amounting to only ~1% of global plant biomass. Therefore, phytoplankton respond rapidly on a global scale to climate change.

Rising temperatures not only affect biological processes but also reduce water density and thereby stratification and circulation, which affect organismal dispersal and nutrient transport. Precipitation, salinity, and winds also affect stratification, mixing, and circulation. Nutrient inputs from air, river, and estuarine flows also affect microbial community composition and function, and climate change affects all these physical factors. The overall relevance of microorganisms to ocean ecosystems can be appreciated from their number and biomass in the water column and subsurface: the total number of cells is more than 10^29, and the Census of Marine Life estimates that 90% of marine biomass is microbial .
