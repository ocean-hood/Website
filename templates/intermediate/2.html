Phytoplankton and its types

Phytoplankton are microscopic plants found in marine and aquatic ecosystems. Just like plants found on land, phytoplankton contain green pigments called chlorophyll, which absorb light to provide energy for photosynthesis. Because phytoplankton need light to live and grow, most are found floating at the top layers of the ocean where sunlight penetrates the water. These plants are also the foundation of the aquatic food web. They are on the menu for everyone from microscopic zooplankton like copepods to multi-ton whales. According to some estimates, phytoplankton provide 50 to 80 percent of the world’s oxygen and absorb between 30 to 50 percent of the world’s carbon.

Types of Phytoplankton

  • Diatoms are a type of microalgae with a shell-like structure that is responsible for a significant portion of the world's photosynthesis, accounting for 20% of it.
  • Dinoflagellates, on the other hand, have a more complex structure with shells and flagella and are responsible for a whopping 50% of the world's photosynthesis.
  • Green algae, which contain chlorophyll, require sunlight for growth and can be found in both freshwater and marine environments.
  • Coccolithophores, another type of microalgae, are covered in calcium carbonate plates and contribute to 5% of the world's photosynthesis. They can be found in both warm and cold waters.

Did you know that cyanobacteria, also known as blue-green algae, are considered pioneers of planet Earth and are one of the phytoplankton? These fascinating organisms are a unique gift of nature, and they come in a wide variety of morph types. For example, there are unicellular species, as well as surface-attached, filamentous colony-forming, and mat-forming species. It's amazing to think about how these Gram-negative eubacteria have evolved and adapted over time to become such an integral part of our ecosystem.

Cyanobacteria have been integral to the evolution of life on Earth, as they were the first organisms to engage in oxygenic photosynthesis. But their significance doesn't stop there. These fascinating organisms also play a vital role in the biogeochemical cycle of carbon and nitrogen. It is estimated that 20-30% of organic carbon on Earth is derived from photosynthetic carbon fixation by cyanobacteria. Additionally, they are the primary N2-fixing microorganisms in marine environments. It's amazing to think about the impact these tiny organisms have on our planet.

Similarly, marine cyanobacteria have a lot of pharmaceutical potential. Recent studies have identified 83 bioactive compounds with potent activities, such as cytotoxic, anti-trypanosomal, antibacterial, and anticancer properties. These compounds are diverse, belonging to classes like cyclic peptides, linear lipopeptides, and depsipeptides. About 43% of these compounds exhibit cytotoxic activity, and 8% show anti-trypanosomal effects. Specifically, different types of drugs obtained from cyanobacteria have been identified to treat various diseases. For example, there are antiproliferative and anticancer drugs such as Lagunamide D, Portobelamide A and B, and Dysidazirine Carboxylic Acid. Some drugs inhibit serine proteases, including Grassystatin D, Grassystatin F, Grassystatin, Jizanpeptin A to E, and Tutuilamides A to C. These drugs have been extracted from various marine cyanobacteria, including Dichothrix sp., Lyngbya sp., Ceramium sp., and Rivularia sp., among others.

The IC50 values for these drugs, which indicate their potency, vary depending on the drug and the type of activity. For example, Compound (44) of Grassystatin exhibited potent inhibitory effects with IC50 values of 50 nM (cathepsin D) and 0.5 nM (cathepsin E). The most potent bioactive compound, (47) of Jizanpeptin, exhibited IC50 values of 72 ± 17 nM (trypsin) and 1400 ± 700 nM (chymotrypsin). Tutuilamides A (50) and B (51) were the most dominant inhibitors of elastase, with IC50 values in the 1-2.05 nM range.

Cytotoxic activity is a key feature of various compounds extracted from marine cyanobacteria. Samoamide A, a compound from Symploca sp., has been found to have significant cytotoxic activity against various cells, including human lung carcinoma (H-460). Odobromoamide, another compound from Okeania sp., has shown significant cytotoxic activity against H-460 cells. Other compounds, such as Keromamide A from Moorea bouillonii, have shown significant cytotoxicity, involving the differentiation of neural stem cells into astrocytes.

It's fascinating to consider the potential of these tiny organisms to help improve human health.
